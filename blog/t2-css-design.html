What are the differences between relative, absolute, and fixed positioning?

Positioning. According to [W3Schools](http://www.w3schools.com/css/css_positioning.asp) “The CSS positioning properties allow you to position an element. It can also place an element behind another, and specify what should happen when an element's content is too big.” Sounds like a simple property, designed to make your life easier. Honestly, it is, but only after you figure out the difference between all four of the attributes and how to properly use them. I'm going to try sum them up as best as I can, because this was the concept that gave me the most trouble, until I realized that the rules were rather simple:

Static

Static Positioning is where an element will be placed by default. If you add something to your page, this is where it will land, & this form of positioning will say, “Now Stay!” That's it. The Top, Right, Bottom, & Left properties can't move a static object, end of story.

Fixed

Fixed Positioning elements are positioned relative to the browser. Set it wherever you want on your screen. Feel good about the spot it's in? Good, because you're going to stare at it for a long while. Think of it like putting a sticker on your monitor – even if you scroll, your object isn't going anywhere. It will go as far as to overlap your other elements.

Relative

Relative Positioning is positioned relative to its normal position. Plop the element down on your page, it will stay there. Scroll away from that spot of the screen, the element will go with it. You can easily reposition them, they can overlap other elements, but that space, belongs to them.

Absolute

Absolutely Positioned elements are positioned relative to the first parent element in the HTML hierarchy, that is not statically bound. Big. Fat. Jerk. Until you understand each other. All of the sudden you are best friends! Directional attributes are totally usable, it can overlap other elements, you can literally place the element wherever you want. A major issue which I had to mess around with to properly understand, is that the document and all of the other elements pretend that it doesn't exist – absolutely positioned elements are removed from the flow of elements on the page.


I had to bang my head against the wall for a while before I figured out how to get each of these to place nicely together, but it is surprisingly rather simple after you grasp them conceptually.
